Response Integrity Warm Networking Playbook
For Doctors, Lawyers, Psychologists, Specialists, Professionals & High-Responsibility People
1. Purpose of the Playbook

The purpose of this networking playbook is to build warm relationships with respected professionals who may know parents, families, schools, or communities that could benefit from Response Integrity.

This is not a cold sales script.

It is a relationship-building conversation designed to explore:

Their professional journey
Their view on pressure, discipline, and survival
Their beliefs about human development
Their opinion on Response Integrity
Whether they know a parent or student who may benefit from RI

The best leads are usually introduced through:

A family friend
A friend of a family friend
A respected professional in the community
Someone connected to parents, schools, learners, or high-achieving families
2. Target People

This playbook works best with people in demanding professions, such as:

Doctors
Lawyers
Psychologists
Therapists
Specialists
Accountants
Engineers
Professors
Lecturers
Principals
Teachers
Business owners
Executives
Faith leaders
Community leaders

These people understand pressure, responsibility, long-term discipline, and the difficulty of becoming competent in a demanding field.

That makes them ideal people to understand the philosophy behind Response Integrity.

3. Core Principle

Do not start by selling Response Integrity.

Start by honoring their field.

The conversation should move through this sequence:

Profession → Pressure → Belief → Survival → Human Development → Response Integrity → Referral

The person must feel seen before they are asked for help.

4. Conversation Timeline
Day 1 to Day 3: Warm the Relationship Around Their Profession

For the first 2–3 days, keep the conversation focused mainly on their profession.

Do not introduce RI immediately unless they ask what you do.

The goal is to build respect and curiosity.

Opening Message

Hi [Name], I got your number from [Person’s Name]. They spoke highly of you, especially regarding your work in [profession/field].

I’m currently learning more about people who have gone into demanding professional fields, especially how they chose their specialization and survived the pressure of becoming excellent at what they do.

Would you mind if I asked you a few questions about your journey sometime?

5. Stage One: Explore Their Profession
Goal

Understand how they entered their field and why they specialized.

Questions to Ask
What made you choose [medicine/law/psychology/etc.]?
At what point did you know this was the field you wanted to commit to?
How did you choose your specialization?
Was your specialization something you always wanted, or did it become clear later?
What do most people misunderstand about your field?
What kind of person survives in your profession?
What kind of person struggles in your profession?
Important Note

Listen more than you speak.

Your goal is to make them feel like their journey matters.

6. Stage Two: Introduce the God / Belief Conversation
Goal

Understand their deeper conviction, not just their career path.

This part must be handled respectfully. Do not debate. Do not force. Do not interrogate.

Transition Line

Can I ask you something slightly deeper, not just professional?

Main Question

Do you believe in God?

Follow-Up Questions

If they say yes:

At what point did God become real to you personally?
Was there a moment where you moved from just following what you were taught to actually believing for yourself?
What changed that for you?
Do you think pressure reveals whether someone truly believes something?
Did your faith or belief system help you survive the pressure of your profession?
Explain the Difference Between Follower and Believer

What I mean is this: some people follow because they were told to obey, but others obey because they believe.

There is a difference between inherited obedience and personal conviction.

I’m curious if there was a point where your belief became your own.

If They Do Not Believe in God

Do not push.

Use this instead:

That makes sense. Then maybe I can ask it differently: was there a principle, truth, or conviction that helped you survive the pressure of becoming who you are professionally?

7. Stage Three: Explore School and Pressure
Goal

Connect their professional becoming to the emotional and mental pressure students face.

Transition Line

That makes me curious about your school or training years.

Questions
When did you go to school or university for your field?
What was the hardest season of that journey?
Did you ever feel like quitting?
How did you survive that pressure?
Was it intelligence, discipline, support, belief, structure, or something else?
What did you have that helped you keep going?
Compliment Their Survival

After they explain, affirm them.

Example:

Honestly, that takes a lot of strength. People often see the title now, but they don’t always see what it took to survive the process of becoming that person.

8. Stage Four: Ask About Why Some People Survive Pressure and Others Collapse
Goal

Move the conversation toward the Response Integrity philosophy without naming RI yet.

Core Question

Why do you think some people survive pressure while others collapse under it, even when both are trying?

Follow-Up Questions
Is it always intelligence?
Is it always discipline?
Is it because some people are slow?
Is it because they are incapable?
Or do you think some people simply never received the right structure, conditioning, support, or response training?
Have you seen capable people collapse because they did not know how to handle pressure?
Have you seen average people become strong because they were trained properly?
Important Framing

Do not make the person feel like you already know the answer.

Let them speak.

Your goal is to get them to articulate the problem that Response Integrity solves.

9. Stage Five: Introduce Response Integrity
Transition Line

The reason I’m asking is because I’m building something in education that is based on a similar idea.

Simple RI Explanation

Response Integrity is an online math mentorship and performance-conditioning brand.

The idea is that many students do not only struggle because they lack knowledge. Many collapse because their response under pressure becomes unstable.

So we train students to stay calm, structured, and confident when math gets difficult, especially under exam pressure.

We focus on:

Clear mathematical language
Clear step-by-step execution
Understanding why steps work
Controlled pressure
Confidence through stability, not hype
Website Ask

I’d really value your perspective on it.

Could I send you our website and ask you to read the “Who We Are” section? I’d love to know whether the philosophy makes sense from the perspective of someone who understands pressure and professional development.

10. Stage Six: Ask for Their Opinion

After they read the website, ask for their honest view.

Questions
What stood out to you?
Does the idea make sense?
Do you think students today struggle more with knowledge, pressure, confidence, structure, or all of them?
Do you think parents would understand this positioning?
From your perspective, what would make this more trustworthy?
Is there anything about the message that feels unclear?
Important

Receive feedback with humility.

Do not defend too quickly.

Say:

That is helpful. I appreciate that perspective.

11. Stage Seven: Referral Ask

Only ask for a referral after:

They understand RI
They have shared their perspective
The conversation feels warm
They seem aligned or interested
Soft Referral Ask

Based on what you’ve seen, do you know of any parent whose child may benefit from this kind of support?

Not necessarily someone who is failing, but a learner who struggles with pressure, confidence, consistency, or staying calm when math becomes difficult.

If someone comes to mind, would you be comfortable introducing us?

Stronger Referral Ask

Do you know one parent who would immediately understand why this matters?

If yes, would you mind connecting me to them or allowing me to send you a short message you can forward?

12. Forwardable Message for Them to Send

Hi [Parent’s Name], I wanted to connect you with [Your Name].

He is building Response Integrity, an online math mentorship program focused on helping students stay calm, structured, and confident when math gets difficult, especially under exam pressure.

It is not just normal tutoring. The focus is on confidence, pressure-handling, clear steps, and stable academic response.

I thought it may be worth a conversation, especially if [Child’s Name] has been struggling with math confidence, pressure, or consistency.

13. Rules for the Team
Do
Be respectful
Ask thoughtful questions
Listen deeply
Make the person feel honored
Keep the conversation natural
Let them speak more than you
Introduce RI only after the pressure conversation
Ask for perspective before asking for referrals
Do Not
Start with a sales pitch
Force the God conversation
Argue about belief
Over-explain RI too early
Ask for referrals before warmth is built
Make the person feel used
Rush the conversation
Sound scripted
Pressure them to refer
14. The Conversation Flow

Use this structure:

Get number through warm connection
Introduce yourself respectfully
Ask about their profession
Explore specialization
Ask about belief, conviction, or God
Ask about school and training pressure
Compliment their survival
Ask why some people survive pressure and others collapse
Introduce RI as an education response-training company
Send website
Ask for their opinion
Ask if they know a parent who may benefit
Request an introduction or forwardable message
15. Core Philosophy Behind the Playbook

The professional is not just a lead source.

They are a witness.

They have survived pressure. They understand what it takes to become competent. They know that success is not only about intelligence.

By helping them reflect on their own journey, they become more able to understand the Response Integrity mission:

Students do not only need more information.

They need a trained response under pressure.

That is what Response Integrity builds.
